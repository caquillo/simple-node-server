Early Protocols

Important dates to remember: 1961 | Leonard Climrock published a paper on packets (which are small pieces of an information) which helped develop the process of sending larger pieces of information efficiently.1962 | A network of computers that can communicate with one another was set up by J.C.R Licklider.

Note that RAND network, a military network by RAND Corporation in America; the commercial network 'NPL' of the National Physical Laboratory in England; and the scientific network CYCLADES in France; togehter with ARPANET, were the foundation of the modern Internet... Anyway, here's a very rough flow of stuff:

ARPANET's Mainframe initializes programs and data files while IMPs or Interface Message Processors were present to take control of network activities as well as act as the interface of the Mainframe, those being the ones interconnected in a network known as IMP subnet. For the first connections between computers, the Network Working Group made the Network Control Protocol (NCP), which later on was replaced by the Transmission Control Protocol (TCP), something that had the capabilites of verifying file transfer.
NPL, as a commercial network, expected a lot of users and file transfer. Therefore, files were divided and sent by small packets, which are to be put back together at the receiver's end = packet-switching.
1962 information systems had a centralized network architecture. The discovery of middle and long rage missiles in Cuba pushed the Americans to decentralize their network architecture so that in the case of a 'node' being destroyed, the network would still work. However, for improvement, a distributed network was developed thus long distances could be covered with a minimum of interference.
CYCLADES, with fewer nodes and a smaller budget to work with, focused on the communication with other networks. They found out that during communication of the sender and reciever, computers only served as transfer nodes, creating an end to end network through the use of a physcial layer installed to the hardware. Phone companies saw this as an opportunity and developed the X.25 protocol which enabled communication through their servers in exhange for a monthly charge.
1971 was when Vinton Cerf created the Transmission Control Protocol (TCP), introducing computers all around the globe to one another through a virtual space. The Internet Protocol (IP) followed right after. After a decade, that protocol was able to send data back and forth.
